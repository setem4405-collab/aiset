## 6. ED-002 Background

`ED-002 — Normative and Informative Boundaries` concerned the risk that descriptive material, examples, explanatory text, or subordinate documents could be interpreted as creating unintended obligations or implicit Charter amendments.

Review 008 assessed this finding as partially resolved.

---

## 7. Normative Boundary Model

The reviewed Charter uses explicit normative operators and structured lifecycle language.

The following distinctions are sufficiently represented:

- mandatory requirements;
- recommendations;
- permissions;
- prohibitions;
- explanatory statements;
- examples;
- historical context;
- future work;
- subordinate implementation detail.

Normative obligations are not intended to arise solely from examples, notes, or descriptive background.

---

## 8. Normative Operator Review

The Charter’s operative language uses terms such as:

- must;
- must not;
- required;
- should;
- should not;
- may;
- permitted;
- prohibited.

The document also distinguishes lifecycle decisions from descriptive status statements.

No remaining operator inconsistency was identified that would create a blocking governance or implementation ambiguity.

---

## 9. Informative Material Review

Informative material is used to explain intent, describe context, provide examples, identify future work, clarify relationships, and document limitations.

Informative material does not independently override explicit normative requirements.

No example or explanatory statement was identified that creates a contradictory mandatory rule.

---

## 10. Subordinate Document Boundary Review

The Charter and current subordinate Review documents establish that subordinate specifications and policies:

- implement Charter requirements;
- remain subordinate to PROGRAM-0001;
- do not amend the Charter implicitly;
- must not reduce higher-level security, privacy, governance, or lifecycle requirements;
- require their own review, registry, and Publisher records where applicable.

This relationship adequately prevents hidden normative amendment through subordinate documents.

---

## 11. Lifecycle Boundary Review

The Charter distinguishes Draft, Review, Candidate Publication, and Approved Publication.

It also distinguishes review recommendations, approval decisions, registry records, Publisher acknowledgments, integrity records, and operational records.

A review recommendation does not itself create publication approval. A registry record does not itself replace approval. A Publisher acknowledgment does not erase unmet lifecycle requirements.

These distinctions are sufficiently explicit.

## 16. Version Relationship Review

Current records identify, where applicable:

- reviewed version;
- previous version;
- revision basis;
- review basis;
- registered version;
- superseded record;
- acknowledged registry record;
- parent Charter version.

---

## 17. Canonical Location Review

Lifecycle and subordinate records generally identify canonical repository paths for the active Charter Review version, current review records, registry records, Publisher acknowledgments, operational Review documents, and operations index.

No missing path creates a blocking discovery or interpretation risk.

## 18. Internal Cross-reference Review

Internal references identify concepts, sections, record types, or related artifacts with sufficient precision for the current lifecycle stage.

Not every conceptual reference requires a numeric section citation. A numeric citation is required only where omission would create material ambiguity.

No remaining internal reference defect was identified that would prevent Candidate Publication readiness assessment.

---

## 19. External and Subordinate Reference Review

References to subordinate documents identify document identifier, version, lifecycle status, review basis, registry relationship, and Publisher relationship.

References to repository history are treated as supporting provenance rather than substitutes for explicit lifecycle records.

This is sufficiently precise.

## 20. Cross-reference Stability

The current reference model relies primarily on persistent identifiers rather than section numbering alone.

This reduces breakage when sections are reorganized.

Where section numbers are used, their function is navigational rather than identity-defining.

This approach is acceptable for Candidate Publication preparation.
