## 6. Finding INDEPENDENT-001

**Severity:** Observation / Future Enhancement

**Title:** Foundation-stage Exception and Delegation Authority Create a Theoretical Self-Approval Pathway

**Affected Document:** PROGRAM-0001 v1.0 Review

**Affected Sections:** 12.11, 13.4, 31.5

### 6.1 Description

The Charter establishes strong normative prohibitions against self-approval.

Section 12.11 states that an Author must not independently grant final approval to their own normative change.

However, Sections 12.11 and 31.5 permit a foundation-stage exception to be approved under a higher-level governance rule.

In the current Foundation Stage, no such higher-level rule yet exists, and the Program Originator is the sole authority capable of establishing one.

This creates a theoretical, but real, pathway for a single person to authorize a self-exception.

This risk is compounded by Section 13.4, which permits delegation of authority without requiring that the delegate be independent of the Program Originator.

In combination, these provisions create a theoretical pathway for a single person to both authorize and delegate final approval authority to themselves or an affiliate.

The mechanism is explicitly constrained and has not been exercised, but its existence introduces a governance vulnerability.

### 6.2 Risk

If the exception or delegation mechanism were used to bypass the independent-review requirement, governance capture could occur without sufficient external control.

The risk is limited for Candidate Publication because:

- the mechanism has not been exercised;
- documented justification is required;
- disclosure and traceability requirements apply;
- Candidate Publication is not final approval.

The risk becomes material for Approved Publication if the mechanism remains unconstrained.

### 6.3 Recommended Action

Before transition to Approved Publication, the Program should either:

- remove the Foundation-stage exception provisions entirely; or
- require that any exception affecting final approval authority receive documented confirmation from at least one independent reviewer who is not the Program Originator.

The Program should also prohibit any delegation that enables a person to approve, confirm, or validate their own normative action under another role title or through an affiliate under their direct control.

### 6.4 Reviewer Rationale

The Charter otherwise provides strong separation of authorities.

The exception and delegation mechanisms appear intended for genuine transitional or emergency needs, but they create an unnecessary vulnerability that weakens the surrounding governance design.

Addressing the issue before final approval would strengthen the framework materially.

### 6.5 Candidate Publication Effect

None.

This is a non-blocking observation for Candidate Publication.


## 7. Finding INDEPENDENT-002

**Severity:** Minor / Future Enhancement

**Title:** Transition Commitment to a Separated-Authority Model Is Implicit but Not Explicitly Triggered

**Affected Document:** PROGRAM-0001 v1.0 Review

**Affected Section:** 13.2

### 7.1 Description

Section 13.2 requires that, before the first final approval of PROGRAM-0001, the Program establish or designate:

- an Approval Authority;
- an appeal authority;
- a conflict-of-interest decision process;
- a registry administration process;
- a Publisher authorization process;
- continuity and succession arrangements.

However, the Charter does not explicitly define a trigger or deadline for transition from the Foundation Stage to a separated-authority model.

Without a clear trigger, Foundation-stage role overlaps could persist indefinitely.

### 7.2 Risk

If the Foundation Stage persists beyond the first Approved Publication, the theoretical self-approval risk identified in INDEPENDENT-001 could remain open indefinitely.

This would reduce confidence in the long-term governance maturity of the Program.

### 7.3 Recommended Action

Add an explicit commitment that either:

- Foundation-stage exception authority expires automatically upon the first Approved Publication of PROGRAM-0001; or
- the Foundation Stage terminates when a formal Approval Authority distinct from the Program Originator is established,

whichever occurs first.

### 7.4 Reviewer Rationale

This would make the transitional nature of the Foundation Stage explicit and provide clear expectations for external stakeholders.

### 7.5 Candidate Publication Effect

None.

This is a non-blocking future-enhancement item.


## 8. Positive Observations

The reviewer recorded the following positive observations.

### 8.1 Normative Keyword Policy

The explicit normative keyword policy reduces ambiguity about which provisions are binding and which are informative.

### 8.2 Separation of Roles

The Charter anticipates and addresses a common governance failure mode by defining distinct Author, Reviewer, Approval Authority, Publisher, Registry Authority, and Maintainer roles.

### 8.3 Candidate Publication Integrity

Candidate Publication integrity requirements embed security and traceability at the governance level.

### 8.4 Governance-capture Controls

Governance-capture and security-sensitive change controls address risks such as coordinated manipulation, fabricated consensus, and concealed vendor control.

### 8.5 Transparent Foundation-stage Disclosure

The Charter openly discloses that one person currently performs multiple roles and commits to future governance maturation.

The reviewer assessed the Charter as robust not only for a foundation-stage project, but also by comparison with many established programs.

## 10. Recommendation

**Outcome:** Recommended with non-blocking observations

### 10.1 Rationale

The reviewer assessed PROGRAM-0001 v1.0 Review as coherent, operationally credible, and governance-ready for its stated foundation-stage purpose.

The separation of roles, prohibition on self-approval, independent-review requirement, and traceability provisions were assessed as sufficient to support progression toward Candidate Publication.

The Foundation-stage exception and delegation mechanisms create a theoretical but real self-approval pathway.

The reviewer did not consider this blocking for Candidate Publication because the mechanism is explicit, constrained, and not exercised.

The reviewer strongly recommended that the Program remove or further constrain the pathway before transition to Approved Publication.

- formal finding disposition;
- completion of the Candidate Publication readiness process;
- registry and Publisher records required by the Charter.

There is no additional condition beyond the procedures already documented in the Charter.
